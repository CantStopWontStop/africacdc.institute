Introduction to Antimicrobial Resistance

Overview

Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) is increasingly recognized as the biggest and urgent threat to global health, food security and development. Though AMR can occur naturally due to genetic changes of microorganisms, the most common factors hastening the process is misuse and overuse of antimicrobials in humans and animals and poor adherence to infection control measures. It is estimated that 700,000 deaths occur annually due to AMR and if the trend continue, over 10 million deaths /yr and over 100 trillion USD will be lost out put globally by 2050. Transmission of resistant pathogens found in animals, food, water and environment is influenced by trade, travel and migration of both human and animals. It is therefore essential for coordinated action to address the challenges of AMR through multi-sectoral collaboration at national and global levels using One health approach.

The primary goal of this short course is to increase awareness of decision makers, public health professionals and stakeholders regarding AMR challenges and control efforts and facilitate their influence on policies, regulations and laws related to AMR. This training will help achieve specific targets in Africa CDC’s Strategic Plan for AMR on policies and laws to enable long-term prevention and control of AMR that aims to:

  1. Increase adoption and enforcement of regulations related to surveillance of AMR at national and regional level
  2. Increase adoption and enforcement of regulations that limit inappropriate antibiotic use in humans
  3. Increase adoption and enforcement of regulations that limit inappropriate antibiotic use in animals
  4. Increase adoption and enforcement of regulations that mandate infection prevention and control in health care facilities
  5. Implement a continent-wide regulatory framework for high quality diagnostics for AMR
  6. Increase adoption of agreement to share data and specimens across NPHIs and other stakeholders

Learning Objectives

  • Understand what AMR is and how it emerges and spreads
  • Describe the burden and impact of AMR across healthcare, agriculture, and the environment
  • Describe recent international efforts to contain AMR
  • Describe the relationship between AMR and prescribing practices
  • Discuss the basic principles of antimicrobial and diagnostic stewardship in multidisciplinary teams
  • Understand AMR and its global impact
  • Describe the relationship between AMR and prescribing
  • Understand how AMR emerges and spreads
  • Understand how AMR affects disease of major public health importance
  • Understand the impact of AMR on health systems and society
  • Know how to conduct surveillance activities for AMR
  • Describe international efforts to contain AMR surveillance
  • Know current global and regional policies and regulations
  • Know current guidelines for AMR control in human and animals
  • Understand the importance of AMR diagnostics, surveillance and data sharing
  • Understand Infection prevention and control in health care facilities
  • Be able to describe gaps and design relevant policies and regulations for national AMR

Instructors

Senait Kedebe, MD, MPH

Dr. Kebede brings over 20 years of experience in academics, clinical and public health practice. She is a global health professional working with international and bilateral organizations including WHO, UNICEF, UNAIDS, World Bank, CDC, and USAID with a focus on capacity building, policy, programming and planning (PPP) for Maternal and child health, HIV/TB, Disease Surveillance and quality of care.

Dr. Kebede received her MD and Specialty in Pediatrics from Addis Ababa University, Ethiopia, and Masters in Public Health from Johns Hopkins University (JHU), USA. Dr kebede is also a Gates Strategic Leadership Fellow for Reproductive Health JHU and certified in Project Management (PMI-2108441) and Health Coaching.

Jay K. Varma, MD

Dr. Varma is the Senior Advisor to the Africa Centres for Disease Control and Prevention (Africa CDC). After graduating magna cum laude with highest honors from Harvard, Dr. Varma completed medical school, internal medicine residency, and chief residency at the University of California, San Diego School of Medicine.

From 2001-2017, Dr. Varma worked on infectious diseases prevention and control for US CDC with postings in Atlanta, Thailand, China, and New York City. He has authored 126 scientific manuscripts, six essays, and one book.

Marguerite Massinga-Loembe, MS, PhD

Dr. Massinga-Loembe is a senior laboratory advisor with Africa CDC, a member of the Stop TB Partnership Global Laboratory Initiative (GLI) core group, and a WHO rGLC laboratory consultant for programmatic management of drug resistant tuberculosis. After obtaining a PhD in Microbiology at Laval University in Canada, she has been active at the interface of clinical research, capacity building and technical assistance to disease control programs in various countries across Sub Saharan Africa, Dr. Massinga-Loembe is aiming at fostering linkage between researchers, policy makers and national health authorities to ensure better uptake of new recommendations, technologies and scale up new diagnostic tools.
